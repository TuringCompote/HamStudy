# Regulations and Policies

Amateur radio in Canada runs on a small stack of legal instruments. The
**Radiocommunication Act** is the enabling law; the **Radiocommunication
Regulations** sit under it; and **RBR-4** sets the operating standards specific
to the amateur service. ISED (Innovation, Science and Economic Development
Canada) administers all of it. Knowing *which* document grants *which* authority
is a recurring exam theme — the Act authorizes, the Regulations implement, RBR-4
specifies the amateur details.

**Your certificate and call sign.** The Amateur Radio Operator Certificate is
issued for life and is non-transferable; your station call sign is the thing you
identify with on air. Keep your address current with ISED. Qualifications stack:
**Basic** gives you VHF/UHF and limited HF; **Basic with Honours** (80%+) adds
full HF below 30 MHz and maximum power; Advanced and Morse add further
privileges.

**What you may and may not do.** Communications must be non-commercial and
non-encrypted — no broadcasting, no music, no profanity, no secret codes, no
messages sent for hire. You may operate on behalf of others and pass third-party
traffic within the rules. In an emergency that threatens life or property, you
may use any means necessary to call for help.

**Stations and oversight.** Rules cover home stations, repeaters, and club
stations, plus your duty to avoid and resolve interference. Radio inspectors have
statutory powers to inspect your station and demand to see your certificate.
