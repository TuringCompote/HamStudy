# Radio Wave Propagation

How far a signal travels depends on frequency, the path, and the state of the
ionosphere. Match the mechanism to the band and you can predict what's workable.

**Three paths.** **Line-of-sight** dominates at VHF/UHF — roughly to the radio
horizon, a bit beyond optical. **Ground wave** follows the Earth's surface and
matters most at low frequencies over short ranges. **Sky wave** is the HF
long-distance mechanism: signals refract off the ionosphere and return to Earth,
sometimes in multiple hops.

**The ionosphere.** Solar UV ionizes the upper atmosphere into layers — **D, E,
F1, F2**. The **D layer** appears by day and mostly *absorbs* lower HF (which is
why the low bands open at night when D fades). The **F layers** do the useful
refracting for DX. The highest frequency that returns from the ionosphere on a
given path is the **MUF (maximum usable frequency)**; straight up it's the
**critical frequency**. Work just below the MUF for best results.

**Solar weather.** Ionization tracks the **sunspot cycle**: more sunspots raise
the MUF and open the higher HF bands. Solar flares and geomagnetic storms can
cause sudden absorption and **fading (QSB)**.

**Band character.** As a rule of thumb, lower HF bands favour night and shorter
skip; higher HF bands favour daytime and long DX when the sun is active; VHF/UHF
stay local except during sporadic-E or tropospheric openings.
