# Circuit Components

This section is about recognizing components — their schematic symbols, what they
do, and how they behave. For someone with an electronics background it's mostly a
symbol-and-vocabulary check; drill the symbols until they're instant.

**Passives.** A **resistor** opposes current and dissipates power as heat. A
**capacitor** stores energy in an electric field, blocks DC, and passes AC more
easily as frequency rises. An **inductor** stores energy in a magnetic field,
passes DC, and opposes AC more as frequency rises. A **transformer** couples AC
between windings and scales voltage/current by the turns ratio — also used to
match impedances.

**Semiconductors.** A **diode** conducts in one direction (rectification,
detection, protection); special diodes include Zener (voltage reference), LED,
and varactor (voltage-variable capacitance). A **transistor** — bipolar (BJT) or
field-effect (FET) — amplifies or switches; a small base/gate signal controls a
larger current. **Integrated circuits** pack many devices into one package.

**Electromechanical and indicators.** **Switches** make/break connections;
**relays** are switches operated by a coil so a small current controls a larger
one. **Meters** display current, voltage, or other quantities — an ammeter goes
in series, a voltmeter in parallel.

The two components whose behavior changes with frequency — capacitors and
inductors — are the bridge into Section 5's reactance math, so make sure their
"caps pass highs, inductors pass lows" behavior is solid.
